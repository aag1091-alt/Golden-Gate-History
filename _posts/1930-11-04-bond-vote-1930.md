In 1930, six counties in California came together to vote on a monumental project that would connect San Francisco with Marin County: the construction of the Golden Gate Bridge. The proposal faced significant financial challenges during the Great Depression, but the bond vote passed by a nearly 3-to-1 margin. Joseph Strauss, the chief engineer, and A.P. Giannini, the banker, played crucial roles in securing the necessary funding. When no other institution would purchase the bonds, Giannini's Bank of America stepped in, making the bridge's construction financially viable. This act of public investment confidence remains one of the most remarkable in American infrastructure history.

**Why it matters:** The successful bond vote and subsequent financial backing by A.P. Giannini's Bank of America ensured the construction of the Golden Gate Bridge, which has since become an iconic symbol of San Francisco and a vital transportation link. It also demonstrated the power of public-private partnerships during challenging economic times.
