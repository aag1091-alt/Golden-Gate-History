# The Safety Net That Saved Nineteen Lives (1936)

In 1936, the Golden Gate Bridge construction introduced a revolutionary safety measure that would save the lives of 19 workers, marking a significant shift in American industrial safety standards.

**What happened:** In 1936, Joseph Strauss, the chief engineer of the Golden Gate Bridge, mandated the installation of a safety net beneath the bridge during its construction. This innovative measure was pushed by Strauss to protect the workers from the perilous heights of the bridge. Al Zampa, a construction worker, was among those who fell into the net and survived, forming an informal fraternity with other survivors known as the 'Halfway to Hell Club.' [Golden Gate Bridge - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge), [Halfway to Hell Club - Golden Gate Bridge Highway and Transportation District](https://www.goldengate.org/bridge/history-research/bridge-construction/safety-innovations/).

**Why it matters:** The introduction of the safety net was a pioneering step in prioritizing worker safety over construction speed, setting a new standard for industrial safety practices in the United States. This innovation not only saved lives but also helped to shift the cultural mindset of the construction industry towards valuing human life above all else.
