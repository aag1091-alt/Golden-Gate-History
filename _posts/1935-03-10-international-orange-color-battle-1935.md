# The Color That Almost Wasn't

In 1935, the Golden Gate Bridge's iconic color was decided, a moment that shaped its identity and legacy.

**What happened:** In 1935, the Golden Gate Bridge's color was debated among various stakeholders. The U.S. Navy proposed black with yellow stripes for visibility, while the Army Air Corps suggested red-and-white stripes. Consulting architect Irving Morrow, however, advocated for the red-orange primer already on the steel, arguing it harmonized beautifully with the surrounding landscape and fog. His vision prevailed, and the board adopted International Orange as the permanent color. [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge)

**Why it matters:** This decision not only enhanced the bridge's aesthetic appeal but also made it a globally recognized symbol of San Francisco and California. The color International Orange has since become synonymous with the Golden Gate Bridge, reflecting its significance in architectural and cultural history. [2](https://www.goldengate.org/bridge/history-research/bridge-design/the-color/)
