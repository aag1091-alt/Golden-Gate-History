The Golden Gate Bridge's iconic design was nearly lost to history without the crucial work of Charles Alton Ellis, whose contributions were sidelined in 1931 by chief engineer Joseph Strauss, with the assistance of suspension bridge expert Leon Moisseiff. **What happened:** In 1931, Charles Alton Ellis, a professor of engineering at the University of Illinois, performed virtually all of the structural analysis for the Golden Gate Bridge's suspension design. Despite his pivotal role, Joseph Strauss, the chief engineer, fired Ellis and removed his name from all official records. Leon Moisseiff, a renowned suspension bridge designer, also played a significant role in the bridge's design. Ellis, however, never publicly complained about his dismissal. [Ellis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Ellis_(engineer)), [Golden Gate Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge#Design). **Why it matters:** Ellis's meticulous calculations and innovative design work were essential to the bridge's success and safety. His contributions were not acknowledged until decades later when historians restored his central role in the bridge's creation. The bridge district formally recognized his contribution in 2007, underscoring the lasting impact of his engineering genius. **Further reading:** - [Golden Gate Bridge – Design and Construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge#Design)
