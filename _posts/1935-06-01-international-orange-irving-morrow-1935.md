In 1935, the Golden Gate Bridge's iconic color was decided, transforming it from a mere engineering marvel to a visual symbol of San Francisco. Irving Morrow, an architect, and his wife Gertrude Comfort Morrow advocated for the bridge's steel primer color, a red-orange hue, to be formalized as International Orange. This choice overruled suggestions from the U.S. Navy and Army Air Corps for black with yellow stripes and candy-cane red and white, respectively. [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge)

This decision was pivotal as it harmonized the bridge with the surrounding natural landscape, making it a striking yet cohesive part of the San Francisco skyline. The color International Orange has since become synonymous with the city, enhancing the bridge's aesthetic appeal and cultural significance. [2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irving_Morrow)
